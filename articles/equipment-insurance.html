Insuring Your Equipment

Heart of the Valley Astronomers

Bottom line up front: Don't rely on your standard homeowner's or renter's policy to fully cover your astronomy equipment. It almost certainly won't.

The Problem with Standard Policies

The average homeowner's or renter's insurance policy (H.O. Policy) covers your possessions, but coverage for high-value specialty items like telescopes is typically limited. Policies vary by company and state, but common issues include:

  • Per-item or per-category dollar caps that are far below the value of your gear
  • Depreciation applied to payouts — meaning you'd receive less than replacement cost
  • No coverage for accidental breakage (a very real risk in the dark at 2 AM)
  • No coverage outside the home — critical if you take your scope to a star party or dark site

The Solution: a Personal Articles Floater

Ask your insurance agent about a policy variously called a "Personal Articles Floater," "Personal Items Policy," or similar. With these policies you specifically schedule each piece of equipment you want covered.

The advantages over a standard H.O. policy:

  • Coverage is generally much broader — including accidental damage
  • Often no depreciation: you receive the full scheduled value if an item is lost or destroyed
  • May or may not have a deductible — ask
  • Coverage typically follows the equipment anywhere, not just at home
Critical: Make sure the policy has a full value clause so that you are paid the full scheduled amount in the event of a total loss. Also ask specifically about accidental breakage — it is all too easy to drop a telescope in the wee hours of the morning.

What to Insure

There's no need to itemize every filter and eyepiece. Focus on the major, expensive pieces. What "expensive" means is personal, but consider anything where a loss would be a financial hardship. A $500 Dobsonian is worth insuring; a $20 filter probably is not.

Items worth considering for scheduling:

  • Your primary telescope and mount
  • Significant eyepiece sets or individual high-value eyepieces
  • Computer-controlled GoTo systems or autoguiders
  • Astrophotography cameras (DSLR, dedicated astro-cam, CCD)
  • Large Dobsonians (especially truss-tube designs)
  • Computerized mounts and polar-alignment tools

Practical Steps

  • Photograph everything. Take clear photos of each item you're insuring — serial numbers visible where possible. Give a copy to your agent and keep a copy somewhere off-site (or in cloud storage).
  • Keep receipts. Even old receipts help establish provenance and value.
  • Update regularly. Any time you buy or sell a major piece of equipment, update your policy. An annual review is good practice.
  • Shop around. Floater policies are usually relatively inexpensive — often just a few dollars per month per item — and rates vary between insurers.

Talk to your insurance agent and get proper protection before you need it. The conversation takes 15 minutes. Replacing an uninsured telescope can take much longer.
