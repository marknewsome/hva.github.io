Night Sky Guide

June Celestial Events

Step outside this month and catch these highlights — no telescope required for most!

Date Time What to Look For
7 Dusk In the west-northwest, Venus sits about 4.5° left of Pollux with Jupiter shining 2° left of Venus. Can you spot Mercury even lower?
9 Dusk Venus shines just 1.5° to the upper right of Jupiter. More details on p. 46 of the June Sky and Telescope.
13 Dawn Look east-northeast to see the waning crescent Moon leading the Pleiades. Mars is further to the right.
16 Dusk In the west-northwest: the day-old Moon, Jupiter 4.5° upper-left, Pollux 4° upper-right, Mercury 3° lower-right, and Venus shining upper-left.
17 Dusk The waxing crescent Moon visits the Beehive Cluster (M44) with Venus shining brightly 2.5° at lower right. (PNW won't see occultation.)
19 Dusk The Moon, two days shy of first quarter, trails Regulus by about 6°.
21 All day Summer Solstice! Longest day of the year in the Northern Hemisphere. Solstice occurs at 1:25 AM PDT.
23 Dusk In the west-southwest the waxing gibbous Moon is about 4.5° to the lower left of Spica.
25 Dusk Look west-northwest to see Jupiter with Mercury about 3.5° to the lower right.

All times are PDT. Check the Clear Dark Sky chart and local weather before heading out.

This Month's Constellation

Boötes — The Herdsman

High in the south on June evenings, Boötes is home to brilliant Arcturus — the brightest star in the northern sky.

Boötes

Boötes (pronounced boh-OH-teez) is one of the oldest recognized constellations, home to Arcturus — a giant orange star and the brightest in the northern celestial hemisphere at magnitude −0.04.

A classic spring/summer trick: follow the arc of the Big Dipper's handle to "Arc to Arcturus," then "speed on to Spica" in Virgo. Arcturus is impossible to miss with its distinctive warm amber glow.

The constellation also harbors several nice double stars and galaxy groups for telescopic observers.

About the Club

Who We Are

The Heart of the Valley Astronomers (HVAA) is a community of amateur astronomers in the central Willamette Valley of Oregon, dedicated to sharing our passion for the night sky with the local community.

We welcome everyone — beginners with binoculars, experienced deep-sky observers, astrophotographers, and those who simply love looking up. No telescope? No problem.

The group meets on the second Tuesday of each month, either via Zoom or in person at the Scott Zimbrick Memorial Fire Station 5: 4950 NW Fair Oaks Drive, Corvallis, OR 97330.

We also run telescope tune-up nights, educational outreach for schools, and regular star parties at dark-sky sites like Adair County Park.
